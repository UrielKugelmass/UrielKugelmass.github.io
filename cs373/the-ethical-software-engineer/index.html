How should an ethical engineer act in this case?

Date: October 25, 2018
The suggestion box:

Your first job after graduation is system administrator for a 200 person privately held manufacturing company. The president/owner sends this message to the employees: "I want to encourage each of you to make comments to me about any facet of our operation you care to. Your response should be made through our anonymizer program so that your identity will not be disclosed" The president/owner finds one response saying: "This company sucks. The only way I find to retaliate for the way I have been treated is sabotage. Every tenth part I turn out is defective." The president/owner insists that you examine the computer usage records to determine the identity of the alleged saboteur. What should you do? You might want to make a declaration of a single course of action. If so, defend it. Why do you believe this is the only ethical response? You might believe that more information is required. What additional factors might influence how to respond ethically to this problem? Even if given much more information, you might be conflicted about how to respond. Discuss your conflicts but do not forget that, given the situation presented to you as the system administrator, you must do something. Even failing to decide is an action.

My response:
I would tell the president of the company (who asked me to look at the system and check the identity of the employee) that I refuse to reveal this person's identity. In addition, I would only continue working at this company until I can find a new job, and I would immediately start actively looking for new opportunities after something like this happens. The scenario that is stated in here is a direct violation of the employee's privacy, or at least an intent to do so, by the owner of this organization. This goes against some of the most important values that I look in a company that I am working for.
In a similar scenario, where the person asking me to violate someone's privacy was my manager, but not the owner or president of the company, I would go to my manager's manager and tell this person about the situation I just encountered.
Apart from the breach of privacy that the company (or at least its president) is intending to carry out, he or she is also lying to its employees. By promising them to not reveal the names of the authors of these suggestions, and then asking a developer to reveal the identity of the saboteour, the company is failing on its honesty with the employees, one of the most valuable -if not the most valuable assets in the company.
The employee, most likely, expressed this comment as a result of the company's promise to not look up the identity of the person making that comment. The employee would have probably said something different had she or he known that her or his anonymity was not going to be respected. Although this is only one decision at a given time, it represents the ideas of the president of the company, that is, the person that has the final say in most of the long-term and strategic decisions made for this company that I am a part of. In a way, these are the company's values.
So far in my internship experience I have had, privacy has always been at the top of the list, and it's been always respected.
I do believe, however, that employees' anonymity should be left aside if the subject matter being analyzed involves sensitive customer information. A lot of companies deal with user information that has to be kept private - examples include medical records, credit card information and others. I think that if a company knows that some employee has been using its access to this private information for undesired reasons, the company can investigate and figure out the identity of the employee (and take some sort of action in response). This is a different case, however, in which sensitive customer information is exposed, and there was no promise on the employer's side to keep employees' anonymity.
I believe that any other course of action - asking for more details such as what made this employee write such a negative feedback would imply accepting that a violation of the employee's privacy is not a wrongful act.
